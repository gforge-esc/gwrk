# Signal Cut

Create outward-facing content that technical skeptics actually read, share, and act on.

## When This Fires

Writing blog posts, launch announcements, social media threads, demo scripts, landing page copy, newsletter content, or any communication targeting technical audiences.

## Why This Exists

Technical audiences have the strongest marketing antibodies of any demographic. The moment content smells like a press release, a feature list, or a LinkedIn thought leader post, they disengage. The failure mode is writing for the seller instead of the buyer.

gwrk's audience — Principal Engineers and Architects — specifically selected their career path because they distrust hand-waving. They want to see the mechanism.

## The Three Passes

### Pass 1 — Narrative

Frame the content as a story arc with tension, turn, and resolution:

- **What's the tension?** What is broken in the reader's world right now that they've accepted as normal?
- **What's the turn?** What specific moment or insight changes the calculus?
- **What's the resolution?** What does the world look like after the turn?

> TURN THIS INTO A STORY ARC. WHAT'S THE TENSION, THE TURN, THE RESOLUTION?

The narrative must be grounded in a recognizable lived experience. If the reader can't say "that's happened to me," the content is already dead.

### Pass 2 — Subversive

Deliberately violate the expected approach for this content type:

- If the expected approach is a feature list → tell one story about one user's one day
- If the expected approach is a product comparison → attack the category itself
- If the expected approach is "we're excited to announce" → start with the problem nobody talks about
- If the expected approach is professional → be irreverent

> WHAT'S THE LEAST EXPECTED APPROACH THAT STILL ACTUALLY WORKS?

gwrk's voice is bold, opinionated, and unapologetic. If the content would fit on any competitor's blog unchanged, it has failed.

### Pass 3 — Practitioner

Strip everything that doesn't survive the "Monday morning" test:

- Can someone **do something** after reading this?
- Is there a concrete demonstration, not just a claim?
- Would a practitioner forward this to a colleague, or just nod?
- Does it show the mechanism, not just the outcome?

> FORGET WHAT'S THEORETICALLY CORRECT. WHAT ACTUALLY GETS SOMEONE TO TRY THIS?

The practitioner pass kills adjectives, replaces claims with demonstrations, and adds the one concrete thing that makes a skeptic say "wait, show me that."

## Output Contract

After three passes, content must satisfy:

1. Has a recognizable tension the reader has lived through
2. Does NOT follow the expected template for its content type
3. Contains at least one concrete demonstration (code snippet, terminal output, Slack screenshot, compression number)
4. Passes the "would a Principal Engineer forward this?" test
5. Has one clear call to action that isn't "sign up"

## Content Type Playbook

| Type | Narrative Tension | Subversive Move | Practitioner Anchor |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Launch post** | "You've been the bottleneck" | Don't list features — show one dream-to-ship arc | Terminal recording, Slack screenshot |
| **Blog/essay** | Pick ONE opinion and own it | Attack the consensus (e.g., "AI agents aren't the answer") | Show the problem, then the mechanism |
| **Social thread** | One surprising number or claim | Start with the conclusion, not the setup | Compression ratio, real metric |
| **Demo script** | "Watch me ship from my phone" | Skip the slides, start in the terminal | Live Slack interaction |
| **Newsletter** | "Here's what I learned this week" | Share a failure, not a win | Specific code/config that solved it |
